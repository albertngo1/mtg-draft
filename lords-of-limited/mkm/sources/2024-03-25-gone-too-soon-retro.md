# MKM Is Gone Too Soon!!!

- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mFajL8fdfhQ
- **Channel:** Lords of Limited
- **Published:** 2024-03-25
- **Set:** Murders at Karlov Manor (Limited)

## Summary

This is the Lords of Limited "50 Takes" retrospective send-off for Murders at Karlov Manor (MKM), the first Magic set drafted with the new Play Booster configuration. Hosts Ben Warne and Ethan Sachs deliver their final, most-informed verdict on the format, repeatedly lamenting that it is "gone too soon" — they felt they had far more to say about it than usual, partly because their attention had been split toward Constructed and the Pro Tour / MagicCon Chicago during the format's run. Their overall verdict: MKM was not an all-timer, but it was a genuinely great Limited format and an excellent debut for Play Boosters.

On the structural level, the hosts celebrate Play Boosters: more rares (multiple rares possible per pack), more uncommons, sprinkled-in build-arounds, and effectively no unplayable commons made every draft feel fresh. The one gripe is that the missing card per pack makes "the wheel" much weaker and adds variance — you really feel the loss. MKM was primarily a synergy format that also happened to have a lot of good rares, and the draft rewarded you for finding the open lane and committing to a clear plan early. Picks one through four had an outsized ripple effect on where you ended up, and the format rewarded delaying the two-color decision while staying deep in a single color.

The defining mechanical pillar was ward 2 on Disguise/Cloak creatures, which made three-mana 2/2s viable in 2024 and warped the rules of engagement — removal had to either trade profitably with a disguised creature or generate additional value to be worth playing. Investigate/Clue tokens were back and excellent (Novice Inspector hailed as a star), Collect Evidence was a rare win for a late-game mechanic that was actually competitive and fun, Suspect played out cleanly on offense and defense though it was a small slice of the format, and Cases were a flavor win that gave nice synergy-archetype rewards without being busted.

On power: white was the best color, then green, then red tied with blue, with black last and fussiest. The top archetype was Boros (RW), followed by a strong Simic (GU) "back again" deck; Esika, Center of the Web was named the best rare in a deep, well-balanced rare pool. The build-around Roots/Chalk Outline decks (insidious roots + chalk outline) and the high-ceiling Izzet (UR) artifacts deck got special love. The episode closes with affection for MagicCon Chicago and excitement for the next set, Outlaws of Thunder Junction.

## Key Draft Takeaways

**Format-level verdict**
- MKM was a great Limited format and a strong Play Booster debut — not an all-timer, but a darn good one. Stop ranking sets as "all-timers"; they never hold up to memory.
- Play Boosters were a win: more rares (multiple per pack possible), many more uncommons, sprinkled-in build-arounds, and no unplayable commons. Every draft felt fresh.
- Downside of Play Boosters: the wheel is largely dead. With one fewer card per pack, cards you want must come in the first 2-3 packs, adding variance — but it makes wheeling a key card feel special.
- MKM was primarily a synergy format that also had a lot of good rares. You almost always ended up in one of two camps: you opened/maximized something powerful, or you found and maximized an open lane. Prefer finding the open lane.
- Draft rewarded delaying the two-color decision: stay deep in a single color, find the open pair, then reap rewards over two more packs. Following the trail from pick one felt good because the format was so synergy-driven.
- Picks one through four had a huge ripple effect on where you ended up. Early gold-card picks could feel like wasted picks (one fewer card in the pack), so prioritize on-color flexibility.
- Decks rewarded a clear goal for when and how you intended to win. Know where you sit on the "end it fast vs. grind it out" spectrum.

**Color power rankings (best to worst)**
1. White
2. Green
3. Red (tied)
3. Blue (tied)
5. Black
- You wanted to start White or Green — those gave the most pivot points. White wants to end the game sooner; Green wants to survive and go long. Opposite game plans.
- Know going in how hard you're willing to fight for white. Whether you get white depends on your pod AND on pack texture (no guaranteed common of each color, so sometimes few white cards are opened).
- Black aggro was real but very fussy/hard to assemble; if black had been better, cards like Snarling Gargoyle Hound would have gotten far more praise.

**Archetype (guild) power rankings (best to worst)**
1. Boros (RW) — best archetype
2. Simic (GU) — "back and so good"
3. Azorius (WU)
4. Selesnya (GW)
5. Izzet (UR)
6. Golgari (BG)
(gap)
7. Orzhov (WB)
8. Gruul (RG)
9. Rakdos (RB)
10. Dimir (UB)
- All ten pairs were quite playable; the format was fairly balanced and anything was viable.
- Selesnya (GW) is a white deck at its core — you compromise more of the green plan than the white plan — but still very good.
- Better signpost uncommons correlated with better color pairs (see Simic). Boros was an exception: its signposts (Lightning Helix, Meddling Youths) were good but not why the archetype was great — the mono-color cards carried it.

**Mechanic verdicts**
- Ward 2 on Disguise/Cloak creatures mattered far more than expected. It made three-mana 2/2s viable, let cards trade early but flip up relevant late, and stopped cheap removal/shocks from generating free value. It warped the format's rules of engagement.
- Investigate / Clue tokens were back and as good as ever. Novice Inspector is a star (the "Thraben Inspector" functional reprint) and is even splashable.
- Collect Evidence was a rare win for a late-game mechanic — competitive, fun, and just hard enough to assemble that it felt special. Multiple flavors (BG, GU splash, roots, chalk outline, cadaver/projector inspector). Even small inclusions (Bite Down on Crime, Vitu-Ghazi Inspector) leveraged it; you didn't need to build the whole deck around it.
- Suspect (menace + can't block) played out beautifully on offense and defense but was a small part of the format — which is fine. Suspecting wrong (e.g., suspecting your own creature with a counterspell) had real gameplay consequences.
- Cases (enchantments that "solve") were a flavor win and nice synergy rewards at picks 4-6, deliberately not busted. Best non-rare cases: Case of the Gateway Express. Case of the Filched Falcon is really only for Izzet (UR). Case of the Locked Hot House has a huge range — sometimes dead, sometimes wins games nothing else could.

**Removal**
- Premium removal was excellent because of the rares running around. Want ~3 efficient removal spells minimum, more depending on plan.
- Because of ward 2, removal had to either (a) trade profitably with a disguised creature (Shock, Long Goodbye, Slice the Shadows, the black cat / Extract a Confession) or (b) give you something extra (Makeshift Binding gains life, Case of the Burning Masks can draw, Bite Down on Crime collects evidence, Pick Your Poison / +2/+0 to enable attacks).
- This pushed some staples down: Murder is fine but unexciting on ladder/leagues (better in high-level pod play); Galvanize is only average (4 mana to kill a disguised creature); Long Goodbye gets awkward late since a flipped creature of MV 4+ blows you out.
- If short on interaction (e.g., Azorius UW), don't be embarrassed to run Dramatic Accusation, bounce, and tricks — you need ways to interact.

**Best commons by color**
- White: Novice Inspector
- Blue: Projector Inspector
- Black: Extract a Confession
- Red: Shock
- Green: Nervous Gardener early; shifts to Lochthwain Eavesdropper once you're in the right synergy deck. Best hybrid common: Dog Walker (Ethan thinks it got slightly too much love; it's the second-best common after Makeshift Binding for him).
- Escape Tunnel should arguably be a top-10 common pick — it's that good for fixing/splashing and for hitting double-color requirements (ahead of Granite Witness).

**Card-evaluation framework & notable cards**
- "Every piece of the buffalo": evaluate cards by how many synergy boxes they check (surveil/self-mill, creatures leaving the yard, collect evidence on, etc.). The more boxes, the better the card. This was Ethan's biggest level-up (the Topiary Panther / Rubblebelt Maverick lecture).
- Good two-drops were hard to find; good four-drops were everywhere. Cards like Out Cold and On the Job fail when you don't have enough twos — prioritize Perimeter Enforcer, Seasoned Consultant, Sanitation Automaton.
- Out Cold > Lost in the Maze (best home for Out Cold is Simic GU). On the Job is better in Azorius (UW) but does a similar job — don't sweat which you get.
- Push // Pull deserves respect: a top early uncommon due to hybrid flexibility; "Pull" ends games and generates massive value. Drops only if you can't cast both halves.
- Lochthwain Eavesdropper was great (a 4-mana 3/3 feels fine in a 3-mana-2/2 format).
- Leering Onlooker is "Lingering Souls on defense" — a great surveil/mill target.
- Hybrid disguise creatures were a balanced win; mostly filler beyond premium ones like Dog Walker (which is notably worse outside RW but never cut from GW).
- Slimy Dualist > Night Drinker Moroi; Slimy Dualist has a real home in Orzhov (WB).
- Cornered Crook > Undercity Eliminator (Crook can go face; Eliminator is just a 5-mana 3/3). Neither is Flametongue Kavu — ward 2 blunts them.
- Overrun is back: On the Job ties white go-wide decks together (premium, made even better by drawing a Clue).
- Connecting the Dots is narrowly powerful (great when it fits, but mostly a Rakdos RB gold card; Case of the Crimson Pulse does it better) — ignore the dumpster 17Lands stats, but if your takeaway is "never play it," you're more right than wrong.
- A Killer Among Us looked like a meme but played like a dream (the hosts overthought which option to pick; Human chosen least).
- Not on My Watch is unplayable for white — wrong timing, opposite of white's plan.
- Vengeful Creeper (green 5-mana 5/5, flips to blow up artifact/enchantment) was great and always had targets; honorable-mention "clunky overperformer." Topiary Panther also in that conversation.

**Rares, bombs & wraths**
- Esika, Center of the Web is the best rare in a deep rare pool. Also great: Aurelia's Vindicator (a mythic, maybe better than Esika), Doppelgang, Esika's Agency Chief (can sac enemy artifacts, not just Clues — give Esika hexproof with Sanitation Automaton), Cryptic Coat.
- The rares were powerful, plentiful enough for everyone, and beatable thanks to good removal — a rare win for the rare slot. Some real "stinker" rares exist (A Fisher's Interrogation, Treacherous Greed, Relive the Past, Case of the Ransacked Lab, Undergrowth Recon), which is healthy tension.
- Wraths were everywhere but fair (No Witnesses, Deadly Cover-Up, Incinerator of the Guilty, Ill-Timed Explosion). They didn't feel oppressive; Clue tokens helped you recover. Deadly Cover-Up is hard to build around — best in Dimir (UB).

**Build-arounds & spotlight decks**
- Build-arounds are back. Headliners: Chalk Outline and Insidious Roots.
- Roots/Outline deck needs: high creature count, the payoffs (Outline + Roots, ideally multiples), and ways for creatures to leave the yard (Rubblebelt Maverick, Gravestone Strategist, Leering Onlooker self-recur; or Collect Evidence / Macabre Reconstruction). Fussy recipe; if a piece is off (e.g., only 12 creatures), it underperforms — but it's a real deck (non-believers are wrong).
- Izzet (UR) artifacts had the fastest, most unbeatable starts (Krenko, Thopter Fabrication, Decimator Dragon, Cryptic Coat, gearhulk lines). High risk, high reward.

**Awards / fun**
- Golden Egg Award (tie!): Grave Titan Strider AND Sanitation Automaton. Strider is the higher-ceiling green/late-game/splash payoff; Automaton is the universal "glue card" with a higher floor that goes in any deck.
- Best fixing: Escape Tunnel (great). Worst nonbasics: Public Thoroughfare (mediocre, best in Grixis-ish UR), Scene of the Crime and Branch of Vitu-Ghazi / Vizier of the Goryo lines (unplayable).
- Clue/equipment "weapons" were a flavor win but a gameplay fail — none worth playing.
- "Imperial Oath" clunky-overperformer award: Crowd Control Warden carries the torch.
