# MKM Draft Battle - Day 1!!!

- **Set:** Murders at Karlov Manor (Limited)

## Summary

Ben (Mr. Metronome) and Ethan (Lord Tupperware) each draft a Murders at Karlov Manor deck on the same pod, then jam them against each other in a best-of-three direct challenge. This is early-format content (third draft for Ben, day-one reps for Ethan), so card evaluations are still forming and both drafters openly experiment with cards they're unsure about.

Ben deliberately tries to stay open and ends up in a greedy, rare-heavy pile. He opens Faerie Snoop, picks up an early Perimeter Enforcer and a Code Breaker, then agonizes over a pick-four Shock vs. Lightning Helix decision (takes Shock for flexibility, later gets a Helix anyway). He collects an absurd number of Faerie Snoops, flirts with a Pride of Hull Clade "toughness matters" build and with green via Axebane Ferox, but ultimately lands on a Rakdos (BR) base splashing white for bombs — Kaya, Aurelia, Wisp-Drinker Vampire, Lightning Helix. The deck has tons of power and removal but shaky fixing, runs 18 lands (with a sideboard of Forests to pivot green), and leans on Faerie Snoop card advantage to dig.

Ethan drafts the opposite: a tight, low-to-the-ground Orzhov (WB) aggro deck built around two-power-or-less creatures and the "go wide" payoffs. He starts Tenth District Hero, takes Lightning Helix, and builds around Wisp-Drinker Vampire, Inside Source (multiple), Neighborhood Guardian, and Case of the Gateway Express (a key payoff he keeps wheeling/picking up). He chases the Snarling Gorehound aggro plan and experiments with cards like Due Diligence and Lead Pipe. The deck has no bombs but a clean curve, evasion via menace/suspect, and good cheap interaction (Long Goodbye, Soul Enervation, Extract a Confession).

In the match, Ben wins game one on raw rare power (Kaya into Aurelia). Ethan takes games two and three by getting under Ben, applying early pressure with cheap menace creatures and going wide faster than Ben's removal and bombs can stabilize, winning the set 2-1. The wrap-up discussion praises removal, white's strong creatures plus Makeshift Binding, Inside Source in WB, and notes the play-booster format kills the wheel — making rares/uncommons matter more and (hopefully) producing a more varied draft than a commons-driven format.

## Key Draft Takeaways

- **Detectives (WU) looks like a top early archetype.** Perimeter Enforcer (W, 1/1 flying lifelink that grows when detectives ETB or flip face up) is a strong common payoff. Detectives at its core wants to be a tempo deck.
- **Removal is premium and you need it.** Lightning Helix is "disgusting," Shock is great. Ben values Shock over Helix at pick four specifically for color flexibility — Shock keeps UR open while Helix locks RW. Makeshift Binding (white O-Ring, gain 2 life) is a great removal spell; Galvanize and Murder are fine playables. You need answers to kill rares/bombs.
- **The big problem with removal in this set:** most efficient interaction kills small creatures, so it's "disparate" — Shock is nuts against aggro but feels terrible against an Aurelia/Kaya. Have a plan for big threats.
- **Orzhov (WB) "two power or less / go wide" aggro is a real, tight deck.** Built around cheap creatures, Wisp-Drinker Vampire (4-mana 2/4 flyer, drains + grants lifelink/deathtouch when a power-2-or-less creature ETBs), Inside Source (incredible in WB; 3-mana 1/1 that brings a 2/2 and pumps detectives), Neighborhood Guardian, and Case of the Gateway Express. This deck got under both rare-heavy decks and won.
- **Case of the Gateway Express is a bomb-tier uncommon for white aggro** and a card you will NOT wheel — it's great with cheap go-wide creatures.
- **Faerie Snoop is a strong, flexible common** (best common in several packs here), but it's mana-hungry and NOT actually great in tempo Detectives. Diminishing returns — there is such a thing as too many Snoops.
- **Smuggler's Copter** is the premium colorless pickup. (Some BR/UR hybrid 3/2 "brings the Copter along" creatures, like Night Watch, are good even without unmorph value.)
- **Disguise/Cloak math:** disguised/cloaked creatures are a 2/2 face-down for 3, flipped later by paying their ward-2 / disguise cost. Cards that are a "3-mana + 2-mana installment" (e.g. Dog Walker) often play better cast for full cost on a turn you have nothing else; you're "cheating on mana" by deploying face-down on two/three only when you'd otherwise do nothing.
- **Suspect** gives menace + can't block — stacking menace across your go-wide creatures pinches the opponent's blockers and attackers (the Snarling Gorehound aggro plan leans on this).
- **Collect Evidence** decks want enablers (Fester Leech is a strong one-drop and self-mills) but need real payoffs to use the graveyard; without payoffs the enablers are just bodies. Demand Answers / similar were underwhelming purely for collect-evidence value.
- **Greedy rare-grabbing is possible but punishing on fixing.** Ben's "take all the good cards regardless of color" plan netted Kaya, Aurelia, Wisp-Drinker, etc., but left him 3-color with bad mana, forcing 18 lands and reliance on Faerie Snoop card draw to find lands.
- **Play-booster format kills the wheel.** You will not get good cards back (e.g. Case of the Gateway Express). The upside: rares and uncommons matter much more, producing a more varied draft than a commons-runaway format. Don't bank on wheeling.
- **White is strong:** best creatures plus one of the best removal spells (Makeshift Binding).
- **P1P1 / signal notes:** when staying open, prioritize the most powerful card and avoid getting married to a color too early; good removal and flexible blue/black cards (Faerie Snoop) keep options open. Watch for color signals via late premium cards (Ben saw late Aurelia, Kaya; a late Ferox pulled him toward green).
