## Summary

Ben Warneke and Ethan Sachs vent at length about Secrets of Strixhaven Limited, a format Ethan says has put him on "full-blown monkey tilt." Their central complaint is sameness: a huge proportion of the set's rares and mythics (Arc of Hunger, the Paradigm cycle, the Emeritus cycle, Mathemagics, Mind into Matter, Moment of Reckoning, Resonating Loot, Splatter Technique, Together as One, Wisdom of Ages) effectively read "I win the game in about seven turns," so games converge on the same inevitable, value-grinding late-game pattern. The format rewards conservative, board-control, make-the-game-go-long play and punishes aggressive lines; it also has so many possible instant-speed answers in each color that trying to play around specific cards is largely futile.

A big chunk of the episode is a card-evaluation post-mortem. Cards that look like slam dunks on traditional metrics — Eternal Student (the rare black 4/2 that reanimates and makes 1/1 flyers) and Eager Glyphmage (the white 3/3 that brings a 1/1 inkling) — turn out to be near-unplayable here because they don't line up with what Silverquill or Witherbloom actually want, and because the play-booster era has fewer one-drops and longer games that punish midrange filler. Conversely, undervalued workhorses like Traveling Minister still overperform. Ben worries that focusing crash-course coverage on commons/uncommons while ignoring rares is leaving them blind, since rares now dictate so much of this format.

On strategy, they argue your number-one goal is to avoid ending up in "bad soup" (an unfocused control pile with finishers but no early interaction). In low-power or mixed-signal drafts they recommend drafting a focused, board-based deck (Witherbloom curve-out, Quandrix midrange, aggressive Silverquill/Lorehold) that meets a power/focus bar, rather than chasing the soup payoff unless you open it early (e.g., P1P1 Mathemagics or an early Wisdom of Ages). They highlight a roster of non-rare cards that steal games against soup decks: Render Speechless (best of the bunch), Ascendant Spirit Speaker (the 3/4 flyer that exiles graveyard cards — a "Rooftop Percher" of the set), Tenured Concocter, Sneering Shade Writer in a drain package, and Root Manipulation.

The episode also covers meta-news: "contender drafts" are panned as a worse value because they share queues and player pools with premier drafts at double cost; Wizards announced that paper card names will match Arena/MTGO (retroactively fixing the Marvel/Spider-Man Omenpath naming); and the hosts lay out a banked-episode schedule covering Ethan's paternity leave and Ben's wedding/honeymoon around the upcoming Marvel set, returning live for the Pro Tour top-eight drafts and then the Hobbit set. They close by walking through several real draft logs, with Ben repeatedly opening P1P5 Wisdom of Ages, and surface a recurring philosophical split: Ben takes the most powerful card every pack regardless, while Ethan favors taking small power hits to stay focused and keep lanes open.

## Key Draft Takeaways

- **Avoid "bad soup" above all else.** An unfocused control pile with finishers but no early interaction loses to good soup, aggro, and midrange alike. It is the single worst place to end up.
- **In low-power or mixed-signal drafts, draft a focused board-based deck.** A Witherbloom curve-out with removal, a Quandrix midrange curve, or an aggressive Silverquill/Lorehold deck can beat a meaningful share of the field. Only chase the soup payoff if you open a finisher early (P1P1 Mathemagics, early P1P3 Wisdom of Ages).
- **Most rares/mythics are "I win in ~7 turns" engines** (Arc of Hunger, the Paradigm cycle, the Emeritus cycle, Mathemagics, Mind into Matter, Moment of Reckoning, Resonating Loot, Splatter Technique, Together as One, Wisdom of Ages). They push you toward the same control/soup shell with the same common/uncommon support, which is why the format feels repetitive.
- **The format rewards conservative, go-long play and punishes aggression.** If your line is 80% now vs. 100% if you wait, wait. Take the slow road and win with better late-game cards.
- **Don't try to play around specific answers.** With so many possible instants per color (counters: Essence Scatter, Disdainful Stroke, Spell Pierce; black: Bitter Triumph, Last Gasp, Masterful Flourish), reads are unreliable — just make the straightforward play, though still avoid jamming bombs into open blue mana when you can.
- **Best non-rare cards for beating soup/late-game decks:** Render Speechless (top of the list), Ascendant Spirit Speaker (4-mana white 3/4 flyer that exiles graveyard cards each combat — a "Rooftop Percher" that wrecks graveyard and flashback plans; must be answered), Tenured Concocter, Sneering Shade Writer (in a Melancholic Poet / Leech Collector drain package), and Root Manipulation (the BG +2/+2 and menace overrun — steals games).
- **Beware cards that look great on paper but flop:** Eternal Student (off-color for what Silverquill/Witherbloom want) and Eager Glyphmage (a near-last-pick). Midrange "4/2 that dies into 2/1/1s" filler doesn't cut it in slower play-booster games.
- **Undervalued workhorse:** Traveling Minister still overperforms despite looking like a weak 1/1.
- **Build decks to match the meta you actually face.** If you draft aggressive Silverquill/Lorehold, run slightly less removal so you reliably present a clock; mulligan hands that don't affect the board (do not keep a 1-creature / 3-removal / 3-land hand). Drawing three removal spells against a soup deck is how you lose.
- **Prepared underdelivered as a headliner mechanic** — incremental two-for-one value rarely decides games when everyone is digging toward a six-for-one bomb ("a knife to a gunfight"). Grave Researcher's reanimate mode almost never gets cast.
- **Color/lane notes:** Black is the worst color to start because it doesn't off-ramp into soup naturally; getting deeper into black keeps both Silverquill (WB) and Witherbloom (BG) open. In low-power drafts, take slight power hits to keep lanes open (e.g., Quillblade Laureate or Wander Off over a marginally better off-lane card) rather than flailing.
- **Read signals/wheels.** Seeing Prismari bombs (Resonating Loot, Dramatic Critique) or a Sarulf late suggests Prismari is open; a strong card wheeling (e.g., Thorn Fist Striker) can tell you the open lane (Quandrix). Being willing to abandon a single good card (Antiquities on the Loose) when you see no support in that color is correct.
- **Removal evaluations:** Cost of Brilliance generally outclasses Killian's Confidence; Stand Up For Yourself is unreliable (can't kill small creatures); Render Speechless is premium interaction-plus-disruption.
- **Mode/queue note:** Best-of-three is strongly recommended here — structural sideboarding (swapping power/toughness to line up, bringing graveyard hate, trimming dead removal) wins games and is one of the format's few rich decision points. "Contender drafts" are not worth it (shared queues/pools, double cost).
