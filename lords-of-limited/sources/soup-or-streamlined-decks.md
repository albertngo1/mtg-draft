# SOS: Should You Draft Soup or Streamlined Decks in Secrets of Strixhaven

- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=96moLNSBajQ
- **Channel:** Lords of Limited
- **Set:** Secrets of Strixhaven (Limited)

## Summary

Ben and Ethan deliver their week-one format dump on Secrets of Strixhaven Limited, framing it as a contest between two poles: hyper-focused white aggro decks (Boros/Silverquill) and "soup" — multicolor late-game decks built to go over the top of everything else. They draw a direct comparison to Tarkir: Dragonstorm, where the format eventually crystallized into Boros versus five-color soup. Both hosts feel they already "see the matrix" in this format: there aren't many undiscovered archetypes, but the drafts remain interesting because each deck wants you to do something distinct, and reading signals to figure out when to commit is heavily rewarded.

A central drafting thesis emerges: in this format the rarity "common" has never mattered less. Ben tries hard not to take a common with picks one through eight, because the gap between the cards that matter (rares/uncommons) and the filler commons is enormous — more commons in your deck correlates directly with more losses. The recommended approach is to take the most powerful card out of every pack picks one through eight without taking power-level hits to get "deep" in a color, stay open, and let a two-color or soup deck coalesce later. Soup in particular should never be forced early ("soup needs time to simmer"); you only commit once you have one or more of the powerful over-the-top payoffs in hand.

They distinguish two flavors of soup: a spell-heavy control build (Prismari-based, e.g. Wisdom of the Ages, Resonating Loot) and a ramp-focused, more permanent-based build (e.g. Mind Over Matter, Mizzix, green ramp into Snarlsong/converge payoffs). Both need early removal to not die to aggro, card advantage and ways to hit land drops (so you're never stuck holding interaction while the opponent draws cards), and consistent life gain (Potioner's Trove) to dodge red and black reach. Black is rated poorly because it doesn't pivot into soup naturally; Witherbloom is a fine mid-range deck but sits in last place because mid-range loses to both aggro stumbles and the late-game decks. Blue and red sit at the best intersection, pivoting cleanly into Prismari, Quandrix, or Lorehold.

The episode closes with a worked pack-one draft walkthrough that flirts with Witherbloom and white before settling into a Quandrix converge deck (a solid "7-8 out of 10" without auto-win bombs), illustrating the stay-open philosophy. They expect the metagame to shift once players stop letting the powerful soup payoffs wheel, and are eager to see how the upcoming Pro Tour drafts differ from the juiced best-of-one ladder.

## Key Draft Takeaways

- **Two pillars of the format:** focused white aggro (Boros/Silverquill) vs. multicolor "soup" late-game decks. A powerful version of either is top of the food chain.
- **Commons barely matter.** Try not to draft commons picks one through eight. More commons in your deck = more losses. There's a huge cliff between the cards that matter and the filler.
- **Pick the best card out of every pack, picks 1-8.** Do NOT take power-level hits to go "deep" in a color. You're almost never trying to get deep in a single color in this set.
- **Stay open until ~pack 1 pick 10.** If you can't jump ship and you already see clearly what you should be doing at the end of pack one, that's the worst-case draft. Don't lock in early.
- **Never force soup.** "Soup needs time to simmer." Only commit to the late game once you have at least one powerful over-the-top payoff. Forcing it without the bombs/converge payoffs produces atrocious decks.
- **Two flavors of soup:** (1) spell-heavy Prismari control (Wisdom of the Ages, Resonating Loot), and (2) ramp-focused, more permanent-based builds (Mind Over Matter, Mizzix, green ramp into Snarlsong/converge).
- **Signals via card "types," not just color.** Notice density of aggressive creatures vs. removal vs. a given college's cards vs. late-game enablers. A density of one college's commons at pick 7 is strong info even if you don't take the card.
- **Color tiers / deck rankings:** Soup #1, Prismari (UR) #2, Lorehold (RW) next, Quandrix (GU), then the black decks (Silverquill WB / Witherbloom BG) last. Witherbloom is a good mid-range deck but loses to both aggro stumbles and late-game decks.
- **Black is weak** because it doesn't pivot into soup naturally; it's mainly good with Silverquill, but Silverquill is better starting base-white. Prefer starting blue/red, or white if clearly open (Jeskai-ish space).
- **Don't fall behind.** Aggro wants to be ahead; soup wants to be at parity. Losing your role's tempo position usually loses the game.
- **Pack card advantage and land-drop smoothing.** In soup mirrors nobody plays early creatures — you lose if you're stuck holding interaction while they draw cards. Run Quick Study, Slight of Hand, Environmental Scientist, the 3-mana land-fetching vehicle, Flow State, etc. Finishing a draft with no fixing lands = bad job.
- **Lands are very high picks once you know your lane.** Take fixing over wheelable on-color commons. Good fixing enables free splashes for premium removal. Two-color decks want ~2-3 on-color tapped duals; multicolor wants ~4-6.

### Over-the-top payoffs (the cards that let soup win the late game)
- **Exhibition Tidecaller** (U, 0/2): mills 3 on each instant/sorcery, mill 10 if mana value 5+. Good in any spell-heavy blue deck; arguably P1P1-level, close to Snarlsong. Don't run it out turn one — time it as a must-answer threat.
- **Wisdom of the Ages** (4UUU rare): return all instant/sorceries from graveyard to hand, no max hand size; best payoff for the control/Prismari soup build. Insane with Visionary's Dance.
- **Mizzix** (UUXX): draw two-to-the-X cards; wins on the spot at X=5, usually lethal at X=4. Best ramp-soup payoff.
- **Mind Over Matter** (GUX): draw X, put a permanent of mana value X or less into play tapped. Pushes toward board-focused green-blue ramp; weak as a blue-red splash.
- **Resonating Loot** (2UR artifact): mana doubler for spells + Library-of-Alexandria draw; goes in the same Prismari control decks as Wisdom of the Ages.
- **Arc of Hunger** (2RW artifact): payoff for Lorehold leaves-the-graveyard; pings/drains and recurs cards. Splashable, synergizes with red burn.
- **Together as One** (6, converge): the "Cruel Ultimatum" plus-version of Snarlsong; draw X, deal X, gain X by colors of mana spent.
- **Sundering Arcane** (6, converge 3/3): exile a permanent equal to converge (hits artifacts/enchantments too, like Abrade) plus a graveyard-bottom mode — a "FTK plus" you want all pieces of.
- **Snarlsong / Arcane Omens / Divergent Equation** are the uncommon-tier payoffs (good but a notch down). If running Arcane Omens, pack ways to rummage/loot alongside it. Snarlsong is better vs. aggro than vs. other late-game decks.

### Fixing / ramp / glue
- **Potioner's Trove** (3, artifact): premium fixing + repeatable life gain; the gain keeps you out of burn/bloodletting range and is critical for soup. Pick it over almost everything once you have a payoff.
- Green ramp (e.g. **Zariel's Experiment** — "stock up at home") and dual lands round out fixing; duals rank above green ramp in non-ramp builds, below it in ramp builds.
- **Brush Off** (2UU, costs UR less vs. instant/sorcery): biggest mover-up — it's a Negate-plus that can also counter real spells. Key interaction for soup mirrors.
- **Abrade** (Brush Off stock up): incidental artifact removal is valuable given Potioner's Trove / Resonating Loot / Arc of Hunger everywhere. Even the white 2/2 lifelink artifact/enchantment-sac creature is filler-plus for this.

### Aggro & college notes
- **Tone Blast** and **Elite Interceptor** are the two (maybe three) commons worth taking early. Tone Blast is repeatable reach that wins control mirrors when recurred (Wisdom of the Ages, flashback).
- **White aggro (Boros/Silverquill):** play great white creatures, threats, removal, and repartee triggers off interaction. It's a strong "LSV cabs" deck when it comes together — but if contested it's a disaster with few escape routes; mitigate by not taking commons early so your first 8 picks aren't committed.
- **Prismari (UR)** is the best late-game control shell; mana-hungry, never runs out of things to do. Key cards: Expressive Fire Dancer, Rubble Rouser, Divergent Equation, Tablet of Discovery (treat as a manalith that taps double for spells), Cenar Unfinished Genius (fixes, finds your best cards, combos with Mica to copy spells via Treasure sac), Goblin Glasswright (filler-plus speed bump).
- **Lorehold (RW)** has an aggro build and a leaves-the-graveyard build (wants Arc of Hunger, Rubble Rouser, the Historian to miracle Visionary's Dance). Different Lorehold decks vary wildly — let your good cards guide the build.
- Burn goes face: Violent Outburst (UR, deal 3 + tap), Rubble Rouser, Tone Blast — soup/Prismari can simply burn opponents out.
- **Cards that look good but aren't:** Pursue the Past (somehow the #1 general-population common, but hosts won't run it — low-power land smoothing), Charging Strike Knight, Campus Composer, Thunder Drum Soloist, Deluge Virtuoso (a trap pick that locks you into blue), and the UR 4/4 loot common Ryan Overturf liked. Eternal Student is a "great card that's homeless" in this format.
- **P1P1 guidance:** prioritize unique, powerful, color-flexible cards (e.g. Exhibition Tidecaller over Burst Lightning because Tidecaller is unique). Prefer starting blue over white when close, since blue pivots into more late-game shells. Note that "can't-be-ignored" PT-relevant commons (Concocter, the RW Opus 4/3) may rise in pod/best-of-three even if they're skippable on the best-of-one ladder.
