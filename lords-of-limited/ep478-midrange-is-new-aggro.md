# Midrange is the New Aggro | Episode 478 | Secrets of Strixhaven

- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OAZ-_Z3V5Bs
- **Channel:** Lords of Limited
- **Set:** Secrets of Strixhaven (Limited)

## Summary

Ben Warney and Ethan Sailor open with a candid "state of limited" check-in. They're worn down by the run of guild sets (two-color-pair sets like Secrets of Strixhaven, the Turtles set, and the upcoming Reality Fracture, which leaked showing only five allied color pairs), which they see as roughly two-thirds the design space of a full ten-color-pair set. Their broader worry: that Wizards is leaning on guild sets as the answer to an ever-increasing release cadence. They credit Secrets of Strixhaven with delivering huge dopamine hits when you correctly read an open lane and pivot, but knock it for an overload of slow, over-the-top "soup" payoffs (Wisdom of Ages, Divergent Equation) that make the format feel like Chromatic Cube as retail limited — the opposite extreme from the curve-pushing LCI.

The core thesis is that pure aggro can't reliably close games in this format. A redundancy of cheap red removal (Impractical Joke, Tone Blast, Abrade, Burst Lightning) plus pervasive life gain blunts low-curve aggression, while Snarlsong gives the slow decks tempo to set up their top end. The answer is midrange: cards that affect the board AND generate value. Elite Interceptor is the poster child — a one-drop that draws a card and disrupts. Blue mostly sits as the late-game boogeyman; the three non-blue color pairs (Lorehold RW, Silverquill WB, Witherbloom BG) fight in the midrange space, with white providing the best disruption tools.

The hosts lay out three pillars for the assertive/midrange deck: (1) nullify the opponent's cheap plays — avoid two-toughness creatures that can't grow (especially at 3+ mana) so you don't fold to Tone Blast; (2) keep pace in the value department — never run out of gas, leaning on Cost of Brilliance, Pursuit of the Past, Primary Research, Group Project, and Stirring Honormancer; and (3) keep the opponent off balance — play around Essence Scatter and Run Aground / Run Behind by sequencing around their counter mana, and sandbag prepared-spell finishers (Quillblade Loriat, Kiroll) to punish the big tap-out turns. The throughline of a quoted veteran's advice: don't draft/play to press your own objective, play to stop whatever the opponent is trying to do.

They close with two draft logs. The first is a powerful Antiquities on the Loose / Scheming Silverquill / Stirring Honormancer opening that towed the Silverquill–Lorehold line; the Orzhov pieces (Inkling Mascot, a WB dual) failed to wheel while Lorehold signals (repeated Kiroll + Wilt in the Heat packs, a P2P2 Lorehold Historian) flowed, cementing Lorehold. The second is a cautionary tale: a weak opening, a misguided Triumph of the Hordes pick (which the hosts conclude is essentially "fog yourself" if it doesn't kill), and aimless drift toward soup with no payoffs — eventually rescued by finding open Witherbloom and trophying with a BG splash of a hate-drafted Cyclonic Rift.

## Key Draft Takeaways

- **Midrange is the new aggro.** Pure low-curve aggro can't reliably close games. Draft "midrange" = creatures/spells that affect the board AND generate value.
- **Three pillars of the assertive deck:** (1) nullify the opponent's cheap plays, (2) keep pace in the value department / never run out of gas, (3) keep them off balance on their big turns.
- **Best deck if open is still UR late-game ("soup")** with cheap red removal + card draw + top-end finishers. But if that lane isn't open, prefer Lorehold (RW) or Silverquill (WB) over Witherbloom (BG); leave Quandrix (GU) and Witherbloom a tier lower for the assertive plan. White provides the best off-balance/disruption tools.
- **Elite Interceptor is the premier common** and the poster child for the strategy — a one-drop that draws a card and disrupts the opponent. Arguably the best common in the set (over Pursuit of the Past among the broader field, though top players still rate Pursuit of the Past #1).
- **Avoid two-toughness creatures that can't grow**, especially at 3+ mana — they fold to Tone Blast and let the slow decks do exactly what they want. Prefer creatures that grow out of burn range (Scolding Administrator, Expressive Fire Dancer, Lecturing Scornmage, Spirit Mascot) or have three toughness (Kiroll, Infirmary Healer).
- **Don't over-commit to "going tall."** There's lots of bounce (Run Aground / Run Behind, Cyclonic Rift) and exile floating around; a single big fractal or pumped Scolding Administrator getting bounced is backbreaking — a knock against the green assertive decks.
- **Pack card advantage even in aggressive decks.** Cost of Brilliance is the poster child; also Pursuit of the Past, Primary Research, Group Project, Environmental Scientist, Stirring Honormancer, Scheming Silverquill, Render Speechless, Poisoner's Apprentice (great in the midrange mirror).
- **Value creatures dodge removal incidentally** — opponents won't waste a Tone Blast on a creature that already replaced itself (Strixhaven Skycoach finds a land; Environmental Scientist). Ben rates Strixhaven Skycoach highly as a 3/2 flyer that smooths mana/splashes; it's worse at low creature counts (10ish).
- **Play around Essence Scatter by sequencing, not by passing.** Don't refuse to cast a creature into open blue — instead, on a counter-mana turn, prefer a Cost of Brilliance, or play a two-drop + two/three-drop so a counter only stops half your turn rather than tapping out for one four-drop that gets countered.
- **Sandbag prepared-spell finishers** (Quillblade Loriat = +1/+1 & double strike; Kiroll = +2/+2 & trample) to punish big tap-out turns (Wisdom of Ages, Divergent Equation, Embrace the Paradox). Render Speechless is a multi-way all-star: take their key card, see their hand, grow your team, push damage.
- **Render Speechless is the card of the week** — a hand-disruption + counter + pump effect that bridges the value and off-balance plans (the hosts wish it exiled, given heavy blue spell recursion).
- **Core principle:** play to stop what the opponent is trying to do (speed up / slow down / close out / advance an objective) rather than just pressing your own plan.
- **Read signals and pivot.** P1 here towed Silverquill/Lorehold; failure of Inkling Mascot + WB dual to wheel, combined with repeated Lorehold signals (Kiroll + Wilt in the Heat) and a P2P2 Lorehold Historian, cemented Lorehold. Early on, take the best card in each pack rather than "tow the line" picks.
- **If you feel bad about your early picks (e.g. a stranded Disdainful Stroke), abandon ship.** Drafting toward soup with no payoffs is one of the worst, fastest paths to 0-3. Disdainful Stroke is great in the late-game mirror but a liability vs. Lorehold/Silverquill.
- **Triumph of the Hordes is a trap in this set** — no blight/poison support means if it doesn't kill, it just fogs you. Don't take it.
- **Take duals only once your color pair is known** (then 2-3 in your pair); don't grab lands while still figuring out your colors.
