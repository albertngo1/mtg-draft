## Summary

This is the wrap-up episode for Lords of Limited's coverage of Secrets of Strixhaven, structured as the hosts' patented "50 takes in 50 minutes" format. Ben Warney and Ethan Sachs reflect on a set they ultimately place in the "dregs" tier of their ranking, agreeing it was fun for roughly a dozen drafts before wearing out its welcome. They argue much of the negative reception was compounded by guild-set fatigue, as this was the third consecutive guild set to open the year, calling it one of the roughest starts to a Magic year since they began podcasting.

The central thesis of the format is "soup versus aggro." Despite being built as a guild set with five draftable color pairs and Mark Rosewater's framing of many draftable decks, in practice the format collapsed into two macro strategies: multicolor "soup" (greedy, Converge-fueled, Potioner's Trove-enabled value decks splashing the best cards) and aggro (primarily Lorehold RW and Silverquill WB). The hosts repeatedly stress that the best soup control/ramp decks were the strongest thing to be doing, beating out the aggressive decks even though 17Lands data favored white because it cannot cleanly represent multicolor brews. White and blue were the two best colors, anchoring aggro and late-game control respectively, mirroring original Strixhaven.

The hosts dig into specific card evaluations. Potioner's Trove is crowned the "GOAT" common, gluing decks together and providing crucial life gain to dodge burn and alpha strikes. Rares and mythics were heavily pushed and defined long games, with Mathematics being a surprise high pick and cards like Together as One (the Converge payoff), Wisdom of Ages, and the Paradigms going over the top. Removal was premium, efficient, and plentiful, but matchup-dependent, making card advantage and filtering (Pursue the Past, Cost of Brilliance, Render Speechless) essential. They critique the college mechanics (Replicate/Rep RT, Magnum Opus, Infusion, Flashback, leaving the graveyard, Increment) for mostly "just happening" rather than asking you to build differently, which contributed to the format feeling stale.

They also run through several mini power-ranking lists: the best Converge payoffs, college power rankings (soup first, then Prismari, Lorehold, Silverquill, Quandrix, Witherbloom last), rares that go over the top in the soup mirror, mystical archive over-performers, and the "I can't believe this isn't great" card from each color. The episode closes with notes on the upcoming Limited Championship Qualifier and a bittersweet farewell to a flawed set, plus reflection that 2026's first half was a desert compared to a strong 2025.

## Key Draft Takeaways

- **The format is soup vs. aggro.** Two dominant macro strategies. Mid-range was almost totally dead. Don't expect the five color-pair "college" themes to define your deck — they happen organically or not at all.
- **Soup (greedy multicolor) is the best thing to do.** The strongest control/ramp soup decks beat Lorehold and Silverquill aggro thanks to cheap interaction crushing their early game. 17Lands favors white only because it can't represent multicolor decks well.
- **White and blue are the two best colors.** White is the best base for aggro; blue is the best base for late-game control. Identical to original Strixhaven.
- **College power rankings:** Soup (effectively Prismari UR-based) first, then Prismari, Lorehold RW, Silverquill WB, Quandrix GU, Witherbloom BG dead last.
- **Soup splits into two flavors:** ramp (usually Quandrix GU-based, more creatures/board) and control (Prismari UR-based, spell-heavy).
- **Potioner's Trove is the GOAT common** — the glue of the format. Its life gain (2/4/6) to escape burn and alpha-strike range, plus keeping a manolith relevant late, made it the format's best card. Better than 2025's Dragonstorm Globe / Starting Column.
- **Rares and mythics were pushed and defined games.** Long games let you find them. Mathematics being a premium card tells you how slow/grindy the format is (you can win by decking and go over the top).
- **Don't pick commons early (the ethos).** Through roughly pick 8, prioritize hits and power level. Open lanes often reveal themselves picks 6-8, so don't lock into a color too early. (Ben softens this — take fitting cards, but never sacrifice power level early.)
- **Removal is premium, efficient, plentiful — but matchup-dependent.** Pack removal, but not too much, since it can be dead vs. creatureless soup. Card advantage and filtering bridge this: Pursue the Past discards dead matchup cards and feeds graveyard synergies; Cost of Brilliance lets aggro keep pace.
- **Card draw/filtering > pure card advantage** because hitting land drops matters enormously. Never stumble; this is a 17-land format, but you want help hitting drops since there's always somewhere to sink mana.
- **The two best soup-fighting cards:** Render Speechless (biggest mover-up, best answer to soup) and cheap protection/interaction. Disdainful Stroke and Spell Pierce shine.
- **Top commons:** Elite Interceptor, Tome Blast (Firebolt-style, a creature-sizing litmus test), and Potioner's Trove are the trio. Then Cost of Brilliance, Rubble Rouser, Unsettle Mockery, Bombard (finally good), Render Speechless, Pursue the Past.
- **Best Converge payoffs (ranked):** 1) Together as One (a Cruel Ultimatum), 2) Snarl Song, 3) Arcane Omens (a Mind Twist — discard 5 is broken), 4) Sundering Archaic, 5) Transcendent Archaic (7-mana 6/6 vigilance). Honorable mention: Archaic Agony.
- **Mystical archive (bonus sheet) over-performers:** Hop to It, Brain Freeze, Disdainful Stroke, Spell Pierce, Stock Up, Abrade. Royal Treatment, Masterful Flourish, and Chase Inspiration are nice cheap protection vs. soup's expensive removal.
- **Aggro must be ahead; soup needs parity; prepared cards punish being behind.** Falling out of your lane loses the game fast. Aggro that falls behind soup is effectively dead by turn 4-5.
- **Take your mana base highly**, especially in college decks (grab your college lands/duals — gold two-drops wheel). Soup decks ran sketchier bases because power-level density mattered more; Teramorphic Expanse and Potioner's Trove provided the omnipresent/five-color fixing.
- **Off-color/off-college bases don't work** due to the plethora of gold cards. There's no clean off-college deck like Dimir in original Strixhaven.
- **Root Manipulation (BG, +2/+2 menace) is the best overrun in a while** — menace beats trample here because creature counts are so low. Witherbloom BG is at its best as an overrun deck, not a grindy life-gain deck.
- **Prepared was a clean, well-designed two-for-one mechanic** (Ancestral Recall, Stream of Life, Reanimate, Rampant Growth on cards), but overshadowed by the four-for-ones and six-for-ones flying around.
- **"Can't believe these aren't great" traps:** Eager Glyph Mage (W), Campus Composer (U), Eternal Student (B — too small-ball value for the format), Charging Strife Knight (R). Eternal Student being bad tells you mid-range is dead.
- **Underperformers from black engine cards** (Cheerful Osteomancer, Necroscribe) — black wasn't usually a late-game base and the best decks weren't creature-focused.
- **Send in the Pest is a terrible Virus Beetle** — wants the opponent in a small game, but games went huge.
- **Notable synergies:** Primary Research + Soaring Stone Glider; Micah, Reader of Ruins + Sonar (treasure to double spells); Zealous Lorecaster + Applied Geometry / Moment of Reckoning (near-infinite loops); prowess tempo with Expressive Fire Dancer + Deluge Virtuoso.
- **Fractal tokens are easy bounce targets** thanks to blue's bounce (Proctor's Gaze, Run Behind, Banishing Betrayal — Banishing Betrayal often better as it's cheaper).
- **Strixhaven Sky Coach** is a two-for-one that helps assertive decks hit land drops and dodges Essence Scatter and Disdainful Stroke (but needs creatures around it to crew).
- **Library of Alexandria is "washed" in this format** — turn-one Library isn't good enough because soup decks go way over the top with engines like Together as One, Wisdom of Ages, and the blue Paradigm.
