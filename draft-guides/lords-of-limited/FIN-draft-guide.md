## TL;DR meta read

- **Balanced, moderate-synergy format** with good lane-finding rewards. Coming in, hosts predicted a high-synergy master's set; reality was "pockets of synergy, not a true synergy format" — the good cards are mostly powerful individually, and removal matters more than initial predictions.
- **Speed: midrange.** Games go long if you pack removal. Four-toughness is the magic number on the ground (dodges Vein's Treachery at base, dodges Suplex). Flying is premium. The ground gums up with tokens and 2/2s / 3/3s staring each other down.
- **Color power (retro, decisive): Blue #1 > Black #2 > Red #3 > White #4 > Green #5.** Green is worst but it's not a blowout — 8 of 10 archetypes are serviceable.
- **Archetype power (retro, decisive): Blue-Red #1, Blue-Black #2, Green-Black #3, Blue-White #4, Red-Black #5.** It's a Grixis format at its core. Green-Red is firmly worst.
- **Mechanic priorities:** Job Select (equipment ETB creates 1/1 hero token that auto-equips) warps removal evaluation — killing the 1/1 with equipment attached is often correct and remains surprising. Tiered spells (kicker-like 3-tier scaling) give Blue-Red cheap spells that can later scale. Saga Creatures are cool but not format-defining outside of Summon Fenrir and Fat Chocobo.
- **Removal is premium.** Biggest format miss was undervaluing removal. Pack 4–6 interaction pieces; Sephiroth's Intervention is the catch-all removal, not a clunky 4-mana spell.

## FIN format principles

- **Removal is premium — pack 4–6 pieces.** The biggest format evolution was hosts going from "removal is overrated, only need 2–3" (preview/crash course) to "load up on removal, 4–6 pieces" (Week 1 onward, confirmed at Pro Tour). Sephiroth's Intervention is a premium P1P1 target, not a clunky four-drop.
- **Four-toughness is the magic number on the ground.** Vein's Treachery and Thunder Magic can't kill a 4+ toughness creature at base rate; Suplex (deal 3) also misses. Creatures with four toughness effectively have a layer of protection. The two most important blue commons (Sahagen, Scorpion Sentinel) both provide four toughness; Namazu Trader (3/4 black) similarly.
- **Kill the 1/1 with the equipment.** Job Select creates a 1/1 hero token that auto-equips when the equipment enters. Despite every instinct saying "ignore the 1/1, kill the equipment or the real threat," the correct play is usually to kill the 1/1 with the attached equipment using your removal spell. This remains counterintuitive even after understanding it.
- **Flying is premium.** The ground stalls out with tokens, four-toughness blockers, and chump-able hero tokens. Anything with evasion gets in.
- **Tiered spells enable the Blue-Red mana-value-4-or-more mechanic.** Tier-1 and Tier-2 costs on tiered spells let you play cheap functional spells early; later in the game the same card can function as a MV4+ payoff trigger. This is why UR's synergy matters.
- **Graveyard matters, especially for Green-Black.** Sorceress's Schemes (the late-game obsession card for red builds) recycles instants/sorceries from the yard. Resentful Revelation fuels the yard. The graveyard was a major resource without the format being Innistrad-level graveyard-centric.
- **Lane-finding is rewarded.** All archetypes (except RG, and to a lesser extent GW/GR) have enough tools that drafting what's open beats forcing a specific pair. The archetype power levels are close enough to stay flexible through picks 1–6.
- **Signpost uncommons are all legends.** Do NOT legend-rule yourself when you flash back a signpost uncommon that's still on the battlefield from an earlier cast.
- **Saga creatures: context-dependent, not format pillars.** They're cool but mostly work in narrow spots. Summon Fenrir and Fat Chocobo are the two exceptions. Don't plan your draft around them as centerpieces.
- **Blitz Ball: not the manalith lynchpin it appeared.** Good as an artifact (triggers wizard pings, ramps, cashes in as a Divination) but doesn't support a five-color-legends deck the way hosts initially hoped.
- **Town lands: don't pick aggressively.** Towns payoffs (Omega, the draw-four-with-affinity-for-towns, etc.) work when you assemble them organically; don't prioritize Town lands early. The draw-four-for-towns is the biggest hidden gem if you end up with a handful.
- **Bonus sheet largely missed** (retro verdict). Notable exceptions: Lightning Bolt and Fatal Push (uncommons) were the two best bonus sheet cards; Lightning Bolt slightly edges Fatal Push. Counter Spell (actual Counterspell) was also real and people forgot it existed.

## Signals and seat reads

- **Format rewards lane-finding.** Once you identify an open color, you often find an open pair. Commit to your lane by pick 6–7 of pack 1; waffling across two completely different gold cards through picks 5–7 is problematic.
- **Double signpost opens = strong signal.** If your P1 is a gold card and you still see the same-color signpost late (like Shanto Tactician Magician at P4 and P7), that's a very strong "this pair is open" signal. Trust it.
- **Late removal = open lane.** Sephiroth's Intervention or Vein's Treachery wheeling is a massive signal. Good drafters don't pass those.
- **No white in multiple packs = white is cut.** Three consecutive packs without a white card = your right neighbor is heavy white. Pivot.
- **Green: need more than one card to commit.** Green requires being pushed into it by your neighbors. Taking an early green card doesn't mean committing to green; it requires overwhelming pressure (multiple premium green cards + the relevant color pair being open).
- **Giving "weight" to picks:** Early gold cards in this format get *slightly less* weight than mono-colored premium interaction because interaction is more flexible. A Vein's Treachery or Thunder Magic gives more options than a gold signpost because you can put it in more decks.
- **Draft what's open and you'll end up in good archetypes.** The only pair to actively avoid is RG. Steer away from green if possible; steer into Grixis colors if the signals align.
