## TL;DR meta read

- **Red is the best color by a meaningful margin.** Every panelist agreed; those who draft red most have the highest trophy rates. Red is deep at common — bolt (Flame-Blessed Bolt), braid (?), epicure (Epicure of Blood), flame breather (Kessig Flamebreather), impulse (Reckless Impulse) — more good cards than you can jam in one deck.
- **Blood is perhaps the best limited mechanic in years.** It smooths mana (avoid flood, avoid screw), enables rummaging for late gas, and unlocks riskier deckbuilding. The panel considers it transformative for the format's feel. Misuse: don't activate blood for no reason at end of turn just because you can.
- **Format is bomb-heavy at rare/mythic but balanced at common.** Commons are close in power level across all colors. Flexibility is rewarded more than usual. Don't default-force a color pair — find the open lane.
- **Best archetypes (settled read):** Blue-Red (spells / counterspells), Blue-White (counterspells / evasion), Black-Red (removal + aggro). Red pairs with almost everything. Blue is the #3 color and undervalued — premium blue uncommons wheel embarrassingly late.
- **Green got a bad rap but is playable.** Big creatures (Hook-Hand Mariner, the 6/6 Flourishing Hunter(?)) exhaust opponent removal. Green is best as a splash/fixing base (weaver blossoms(?)) rather than a streamlined 2-color beatdown. Green-Red is the cleanest green 2-color option. Green-White training is considered weak by most panelists.
- **Werewolves are real.** Unlike Midnight Hunt, double-spelling is harder here, so flipping werewolves is easier. Don't undervalue the archetype as you would have in MID.
- **17 or 18 lands.** High-curve decks (especially with blood) frequently want 18. Blood smooths this out.

## Format principles

1. **Red is the best color; hard-force it if signals allow.** Start every draft looking for red signals (bolt, braid, flame breather). Only pivot when red is clearly cut. Jacob's heuristic: "only when it's abundantly clear red is not open do I shift."

2. **Blood is the mechanic that defines the format.** It lubricates your mana, helps you find answers, and enables riskier card choices. Don't waste blood tokens — don't rummage for no reason. Cards that generate blood are fundamentally more valuable than they look on paper.

3. **Flexibility beats hard-forcing a color pair.** Unlike recent formats (AFR, MID, STX) there is no one dominant color pair you must be in. Draft colors first, confirm color pair late. Stay open through pick 6-8 of pack 1. You can pivot as late as pack 3 if a bomb appears.

4. **Stay in one color early, find the second color later.** Typical approach: commit to red (or your best open color) in pack 1; let pack 2's signals confirm the second color. Alex explicitly advises staying theoretically open to every color pair until late in the draft.

5. **Bombs warp the format but don't break it.** Every bomb has an answer at common or uncommon (sanctifier(?), bleed dry(?), counterspells). In best-of-three, sideboard silver bullets. In best-of-one, design the deck to either beat bombs proactively (get under them) or answer them reactively (counterspells, removal package).

6. **Blue is undervalued — blue uncommons wheel.** Biolume Egg(?), scab-diver (diver scob?), and counterspells (siphon essence, syncopate, geist-light snare) wheel at pick 6-8, sometimes later. If you're in blue, draft the package intentionally even if the egg is your only exploiter payoff.

7. **Counters are much better than they appear.** Modern counter spells do things beyond countering (give blood tokens, exile, give zombie tokens). Jim/Goosey reports that counterspell-heavy draws won a very high fraction of his trophy matches.

8. **Big creatures exhaust removal.** Anything above 4/4 stat-line typically demands a spell answer, not a creature answer. This is why green's Hook-Hand Mariner and Flourishing Hunter(?) punch above their archetype reputation — they force opponents to use hard removal which clears the path for bombs.

9. **Life gain is better than it looks.** Chris/florida-mon flags this as one of his biggest format surprises. Life gain in white and black buys turns to find bombs, stabilizes against aggro, and enables Boros-style mirrors where you attack fearlessly.

10. **17-18 lands.** High-curve blood decks often want 18. Blood mitigates flood. Lower curves can run 17. The showdown episode specifically notes "100% playing 18 lands in this deck" with a high-curve blue-red build that makes little blood.

11. **Two-drops are less important than usual.** Alex notes he adjusted by taking two-drops less highly and removal more highly versus his habits from previous formats. The large-creature environment means cheap 2-drops don't scale into mid-game well.

12. **Know how your deck wins before committing to picks.** Jacob emphasizes: even with similar card types, different decks win differently. If your deck has 1 Kessig Flamebreather and 4 angers(?) but lacks early damage, you need a plan for the long game (e.g. bring in bloody betrayal(?) or act of freezing(?) as finishers).

13. **Werewolves are underrated.** Double-spelling is much harder in VOW than MID (fewer flash tricks, fewer "play two spells" enablers, no silver bolt artifacts at common). Werewolves flip reliably. Don't automatically skip the archetype as MID habits would dictate.

## Signals & seat reads

- **Red is contested.** Beers notes it clearly and Ethan confirms: red runs deep but everyone wants it, so expect it to be cut sometimes. Read your seat by pack 1, picks 4-6 to see how much red is flowing.
- **Blue wheeling signals open blue.** Counterspells (syncopate, siphon essence, geist-light snare), and especially Biolume Egg going 6th-7th pick, means blue is open. The consensus is blue is undervalued enough that these premium uncommons regularly wheel.
- **White going late means go white.** Traveling Minister, Ancestor's Embrace, etc. at pick 5-6 suggest white is open.
- **Green usually signals late by nature.** Flourishing Hunter and Hook-Hand Mariner going 7th+ is not unusual — green just doesn't attract drafters early. Don't over-read this as green being open if it's just inherently underdrafted.
- **If you see an estate (Voldaren Estate?) in pack 1, few bombs were opened** — Ethan notes this directly. Lower-bomb packs push toward more commons-based strategies.
- **The "backdoor" heuristic** (Jim): keep an eye on a fallback 2-color lane throughout pack 1. For red-focused drafters: blue-red is the primary plan, blue-white is the emergency fallback. Those backdoor cards wheeling confirms the lane.
- **Don't over-signal-chase.** Alex warns against mentally locking in: "don't count yourself out as any color pair until the end of the draft." Flexibility lets you pick up late-pack bombs that define the deck.
- **Black being cut late in the format** — Ethan notes near Dec 1 that black was feeling cut, possibly because people are holding strong black rares (Blood Vial Purveyor(?), Dreadfeast Demon, Toxrill) from pack-1 first-picks.

## Supersessions (early-was-wrong, now corrected)

- **Blue-Black Exploit (early take → settled):** beers initially thought "blue-black was the broken deck of the format at first" and went deep on the exploit plan. He received pushback (from Ethan on stream) that exploit was not where you wanted to be. After more reps, beers confirms: exploit is not the place to be. **Verdict: exploit is mediocre; don't force it.** Early (week 1) → Corrected (week 3+, roundtable Dec 1).
- **Blue's overall rating (early → settled):** Blue got a "bad rap early" in the format (per multiple roundtable panelists). Both Jim and Alex confirm that by mid-format, blue has emerged as a strong support color — counterspells over-perform, biolume egg/scab-diver go embarrassingly late, and blue-red/blue-white are among the best archetypes. **Verdict: blue is undervalued; treat it as #3 color.** Early (first weekend) → Corrected (by ~week 2-3).
- **Werewolves dismissed from MID habits:** Early VOW drafters dismissed werewolves, importing the lesson from Midnight Hunt where they were unreliable. Multiple roundtable panelists (Jim explicitly) note this was wrong — double-spelling is much harder in VOW, so werewolves flip reliably and were "data-wise among the best decks" early in the format. **Verdict: werewolves are real; don't pre-dismiss them.** Pre-format habit → Corrected early in VOW (roundtable Dec 1).

## Live disagreements (genuine, unresolved)

- **How much to prioritize red specifically vs. drafting open.** beers/Jacob are hard red-first drafters; ncaa and Alex are "draft best cards, find open lane" drafters who prefer red but don't hard-bias toward it. Both strategies have produced trophy rates well above average, so the disagreement is unresolved.
- **Flame Breather — bomb or not?** beers would consider taking Kessig Flamebreather over Avabruck Caretaker (top green rare/bomb) because of his comfort in the strategy. Jim calls this "a reach." Both acknowledge it's a stylistic/comfort disagreement. beers' 76% win rate supports his approach empirically for his playstyle, but the panelists are not universally convinced.
- **Value of green-blue streamlined vs. green as splash.** ncaa/Alex defend green-blue as viable. Jim reports 1 green-blue draft in 100 total (went 0-2), thinks the gold card (Biolume Egg?) isn't pulling the pair together and the spider(?) needed to be a mana ramper. Most panelists agree green is best as splash base; whether green-blue is specifically viable as a 2-color plan remains disputed.
- **Blocking philosophy.** beers says never block, always maintain pressure. Jim says blocking is good (for the counter-spell strategy). Both are trophying at elite rates. Ethan jokingly agrees with Jim but the panel doesn't resolve it.
