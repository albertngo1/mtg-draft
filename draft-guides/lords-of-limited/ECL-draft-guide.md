## TL;DR meta read

- **Elves is the best deck in the format** (settled read, 50-takes retro). It is the best deck in *both* the early and late game — strong creature sizing + graveyard synergy via Morcan's Eyes. Merfolk is second, Elementals third (sleeper that kept rising), Goblins fourth, Kithkin last among the five typal pairs.
- **The format has 10+ real archetypes** if you look past the surface tribal structure. The five typal two-color pairs (Elves, Merfolk, Elementals, Goblins, Kithkin) are all real; so are two-color Vivid, Black-White Blight, Blue-Black Fairies/Flash, and Red-White Blight. **Non-typal decks are real and fun, but you were incentivized to be in a typal pair** because it unlocks gold lords, Eclipsed cards, and commands — more good cards flowing through your seat.
- **Vivid is the sixth linear deck** and the hosts' sleeper call. Best built as strictly two-color (never five-color piles); the four key payoffs are Shine Striker, Prisma Basher, Explosive Prodigy, and Kith Keeper. The vivid deck loves a board stall and crushes the late game when ahead.
- **Blight is genuinely upside when you build for it** (retro confirms). Blight-one in particular is almost free — enables Cinder Strike as a one-mana deal-four and unlocks Moonlit Mentor, Reaping Willow, and other blight payoffs. Blight is NOT just upside by default; it only becomes upside when you build your deck to treat counters as resources.
- **Format speed:** *slower than expected*. Two-drops are plentiful in draft but often get blanked by high-toughness bodies. Plays often start on turn three in practice. Playing off-curve and holding removal for key threats was rewarded. **Five toughness** is the magic number — dodges Blight Rot, Seer, and Cinder Strike on blight mode.
- **Removal is important but not spammy.** Do NOT use removal to push damage; save it for key threats (lords, engines). Holding up flash creatures and removal is a legitimate game plan.
- **Creature counts run high (15–20)** across all archetypes — tribal, vivid, and blight all want boards of colored-pip permanents. Curve-filler commons with no synergy are dead weight; be disciplined.
- **Card advantage wins the resource war.** Unexpected Assistance (draw-3 convoke), Burning Curiosity, Mist Meadow Council, and Flaring Cinder (via rummage) are key engines. The format goes long; whoever runs out of cards first loses.

## Format principles

**Draft philosophy:**
- You are highly incentivized to be in one of the five supported typal color pairs. Being in a supported pair means your seat unlocks lords, eclipsed cards, commands, and double-faced rares — more good cards in your direction. Being in a non-typal pair does not get those.
- That said, vivid (two-color), blight (BW or RB), and fairies/flash (UB) are real off-ramps if you get cut.
- The HAIL method the hosts developed for this format: **H**aymakers (bombs, pushed uncommons) → **A**nswers (Cinder Strike, Bog Slither's Embrace, Seer, Luminal/Leenal Hold) → **I**dentity cards (signpost lords, eclipsed cards) → **L**everage (Unexpected Assistance, Burning Curiosity, Mist Meadow Council). Take haymakers and answers early; identity cards later once your lane is clear.
- **Delay the decision using changelings and hybrid cards.** Changelings are better than lessons or allies from other formats because they satisfy typal payoffs, count for vivid pips, work in multiple decks, and are great in draft to keep your options open. Mischievous Sneakling is the best of the bunch; Changeling Wayfinder is excellent ramp/fixing.
- **Don't take power-level hits to go deep in a tribe early.** Taking a C-level goblin card over a B-minus flexible card early is wrong. Take the best card and let the lane reveal itself.
- **Eclipsed uncommons were overrated by the community** (retro verdict). They are conditional: great if you have 12+ hits for them to find; mediocre if you only have 4. Mist Meadow Council and Unexpected Assistance provide similar "dig" without a density requirement.
- **Do NOT draft bad versions of typal decks.** A contested tribal lane means your deck will be atrocious. If you're one of two Kithkin drafters, your deck is bad. Read the signals early — if the good tribal cards dry up, pivot to vivid or blight.

**Deckbuilding:**
- **Creature counts should be 15–20.** All strategies need board presence — even vivid needs colored pips in play to trigger payoffs.
- **Two-drop slot is tricky.** Two-drops often get blanked by high-toughness bodies. Only play two-drops that are synergistic cogs in your specific strategy. A two-mana 2/2 elf without elves synergy is very weak.
- **Curve often starts on three**, not two. Don't panic-fill the two-drop slot with filler.
- **Vivid mana base:** strictly two-color. Use hybrid cards (Nogggle Robber, Eclipse Kithkin/Flamekin, Wary Farmer, Prideful Feastling) + Shimmer Wild's Growth + Puka's Eye to raise vivid count without a bad mana base. Do NOT play five-color piles — you fill your deck with fixing and run out of interaction.
- **Ramp in vivid decks:** Shimmer Wild's Growth and Nogggle Robber (treasure) are how you spike Prisma Basher a turn early. Prismatic Undercurrents is only good if your vivid count reliably hits three when you cast it — rarely the case in true two-color builds.
- **Incidental artifact/enchantment removal in the main deck.** There are tons of targets: Spiral into Solitude, Blossom Bind, Luminal Hold (aura removal), Gathering Stone, Dawnblessed Penitent before they crack it. Keep Out is fine as a one-of.

**Gameplay:**
- **Hold removal for key threats** — do NOT fire removal to push one or two points of damage. Save it for lords, engines, and flash finishers.
- **Five toughness dodges the three main removal spells** (Blight Rot puts four -1/-1 counters, Seer deals four, Cinder Strike with blight deals four). Cards with 5 toughness are functionally premium.
- **Do not slam attacks early.** Flash creatures (especially Mischievous Sneakling, Glamour Gifter, and reach creatures) punish early attacks into open mana. Buyer beware — this format has more flash-density than usual.
- **Playing off-curve is rewarded.** It is correct in this format to hold up your flash two-drop rather than slam it on turn two, or to use your two mana on a removal spell instead of your three-drop on curve.
- **Resource war matters.** With slower games, discarding a card from Dream Seeker ETB and drawing three from Unexpected Assistance are massive swings. Deck-advantage is often the deciding factor in late turns.

## Signals & seat reads

- **Sealed lanes telegraph what's open.** The format is self-correcting: if Kithkin is the best deck, everyone knows to draft Kithkin, so it'll be contested. Read the table quickly.
- **Early picks of lords and Eclipsed cards in your colors signal open lanes.** If you see a Deep Channel Duelist pick four or a Morcan's Loyalist late in pack one, Merfolk or Elves is open. Commit.
- **What wheels:** Blight-themed build-arounds (Blighted Blackthorn, Narho Bark Elm) go very late — Blighted Blackthorn was often a pick-10 wheel. Vivid payoffs (Prisma Basher, Kith Keeper) also go late because most decks don't want them. Pick these up as free additions once your plan is set.
- **Two-drops in contested colors will be scarce.** If you're one of two drafters in a typal deck, the relevant two-drops get fought over. The bad version of your deck plays two-drops that are off-type, and those get blanked.
- **Hybrid cards are signals.** If you see late Mischievous Sneaklings or Prideful Feastlings, someone upstream passed them — probably not in those colors. Good time to consider Fairies or BW pivots.
- **Blight decks as off-ramps:** If you start Kithkin and get pushed off, look for a white-black or red-black blight pivot. The blight cards (Bog Slither's Embrace, Cinder Strike, Bog Slither's Embrace, Reaping Willow) go later than the tribal pieces and are still excellent. "Blight is a good off-ramp."
- **Elemental lane undervalued by community.** Elementals cards went late throughout the format — land cyclers, Flamebraider, and Flaring Cinder were wheeling even in mid-format. If you want to force vivid or Elementals, you will see cards.

## Supersessions (early-was-wrong, now corrected)

These are genuine reversals where a later episode contradicts an earlier take:

1. **Kithkin tier:** *Early (cc, 2026-01-12):* "Kithkin is the #1 boogeyman tribe; curveouts look absolutely nasty." → *Late (50t, 2026-03-23):* Kithkin finished **dead last** among the five tribal pairs. "There was just too much lifegain/cheap interaction/defensive speed for this deck to really get there." Verdict: **Late take wins — Kithkin is C-tier.**

2. **Eclipsed uncommons value:** *Early (ea, 2026-01-19):* Both hosts treated eclipsed tribal uncommons as strong signal cards, roughly B+ picks. → *Late (50t, 2026-03-23):* "The cycle of eclipsed uncommons were overrated by the community. We were probably more wrong than the community was wrong." The hosts acknowledge they resisted this truth for too long. Verdict: **Late take wins — Eclipsed cards are conditional, not universally B+.**

3. **Narho Bark Elm:** *Early (cc):* Ethan flagged it as exciting blight payoff. Ben made it top-3 black uncommon. → *Late (ea + retro):* "Three mana 1/2 is really bad. Glacially slow." Biggest fall-from-grace nominee. Verdict: **Late take wins — cut this card.**

4. **Gravelgill Scoundrel:** *Early (cc):* Ben explicitly gave it a **zero** (worst possible grade). → *Late (50t):* "Another terrible look for me. That card rose to one of the most important Merfolk commons." "Racing with Scoundrel plus Bark of Doran makes a 4/4 unblockable vigilance." Verdict: **Late take wins — strong Merfolk common.**

5. **Format complexity / "only five decks":** *Early (cc, ea):* Both hosts worried the format would be boring, just five tribal paint-by-numbers decks. → *Late (50t):* "Lorwyn block had 10 or more archetypes if you dug past the surface." Vivid, blight, and fairies added genuine depth. Verdict: **Nuanced — it WAS heavily incentivized to be typal (the data proves it), but non-typal decks were real.**
