## TL;DR meta read

- **Graveyard-matters format with multiple overlapping synergy axes.** The best decks are built around Delirium, Manifest Dread, or Eerie (enchantments/rooms), and all three reward having a stocked graveyard. Green and Black are the strongest colors; green in particular is deep at both common and uncommon.
- **Format speed: midrange, not aggro.** Five- and six-drop bomb uncommons are legitimate P1P1 considerations. You likely cannot keep hands without a turn-two play on the draw, but the engine-based color pairs dominate once they "turn on." Red-White (aggro, power-two-or-less matters) is supported but shallow and isolated from the best mechanics.
- **Best colors: Green > Black >> Blue > White > Red** (Crash Course and confirmed mid-format). Green has the best commons AND uncommons. Black has premium removal and payoffs. Blue has weak commons but strong uncommons. Red is deep in archetypes but its single-color cards are weakest.
- **Best archetypes (settled read): BG Delirium #1, UG Manifest Dread #2, BW Reanimator #3, BG/x Graveyard Soup #4.** Eerie decks (Blue-White, Blue-Black) are real and can be great in open lanes. Red-White is real but shallow; Blue-Red Rooms is inconsistent.
- **Key mechanics:** Manifest Dread (put creature face-down, may flip up; fuels graveyard), Delirium (4+ card types in yard; huge payoffs), Eerie (enchantments and fully unlocked Rooms trigger bonus effects), Survival (tap to trigger during second main phase), Rooms (double-sided enchantment lands that you unlock over time).
- **Removal notes:** Four toughness is the "magic number" — above the range of Scorching Dragonfire (1R: deal 3) and Nowhere to Run (1B flash: −3/−3). Exiling removal matters: putting things in graveyards is often a BENEFIT for opponents in this format, so exile or tuck effects are premium.
- **Terramorphic Expanse is a notable common** — provides fixing AND puts a land type in the graveyard for Delirium, plus has artifact type. A top-10 common per both hosts.

## Format principles

- **Green is busted.** Both hosts called this out in the Crash Course and confirmed it throughout. Green has elite commons (Spine Seeker Centipede, Say Its Name, Monster Emergence, Manifest Dread the sorcery) and elite uncommons (Threats Around Every Corner, Under the Skin, Wickerbough Thresher, Moldering Gym/Wait Room). If you're playing green, you're probably playing great cards.

- **Four toughness is the magic number.** Scorching Dragonfire (1R: deal 3, exile if dies) and Nowhere to Run (1B flash: −3/−3) are the premier "damage-based" removal spells. Being above both dodges a huge swath of removal. Cards like Skullcap Nuisance (1/4) and Brood Weaver (2/3) are valued partly for surviving these.

- **Exiling vs. killing matters a lot.** Putting things into graveyards helps Delirium and Reanimator opponents. Exile and library-tuck effects are premium (Trapped in the Screen, Scorching Dragonfire's death clause, Sheltered by Ghosts, Unable to Scream). Avoid kill spells that send things to the yard when opponents want that.

- **Graveyard types for Delirium:** You need four different card types among cards in your graveyard. Artifacts are the hardest type to get naturally — Glimmerlight (artifact equipment), Terramorphic Expanse (artifact land), Piggy Bank, Patchwork Beastie, and Clockwork Percussionist are all flagged as delirium "glue." Say Its Name (gets sorcery, creature/land into yard in one shot) is a top-10 common specifically for this reason.

- **Manifest Dread is the defining mechanic.** Multiple cards across every color interact with it. When you manifest, you may (a) put a face-down 2/2 onto the battlefield that you can flip up for its mana cost, and (b) put the other card into your graveyard to progress Delirium. Blue-Green in particular synergizes with flipping face-down creatures and getting those graveyard cards back.

- **Rooms are a unique deckbuilding consideration.** Rooms are double-sided enchantment cards that enter attached to your mana base and can be "unlocked" for their effects. They count as enchantments for Eerie triggers and as permanents that left the battlefield (e.g. for Defiled Crypt/Cadaver Lab triggers). Building around them requires commitment; Ghostly Key Bearer enables room synergies cleanly.

- **Archetype bleed is real — plan your splash early.** Green gets extra fixing (Spine Seeker Centipede, Moldering Gym, green fight cards, Threats Around Every Corner that grabs basics) on top of the colorless fixing available to everyone (Terramorphic Expanse, land cyclers). Three-color "Jund delirium" or "Abzan reanimator" or "Sultai graveyard" are all legitimate endgames to aim for. Red-White is the one archetype that does NOT want to splash and benefits least from the splash options.

- **Timing your pivot:** The hosts repeatedly discuss that starting a color-pair commitment too early can trap you in a weak seat. They favor taking powerful mono-colored cards early (especially removal, efficient creatures) and waiting until pack 2 or mid-pack-1 to commit to an archetype. Exception: if you open a signpost bomb and get signals it's wide open, commit immediately.

- **Fear of Falling (3UU: 4/4 flyer, attack gives something −2/−0 and loses flying) was overrated week 1.** By the mid-format episodes, both hosts had stepped back significantly: "I've resolved that card and it just gets killed for one or two mana and I can't recover from the tempo loss." Five-drop flyers that don't immediately impact the board are much weaker than they look.

- **Monstrous Emergence (1G fight spell) gets mixed reviews.** Ben: "I hate it" by mid-format, noting it doesn't exile and green decks often run 11–12 creatures (which can be awkward). Ethan was higher. It's functional but not premium removal — Coordinated Clobbering (tap creatures to pump) is called out as actively bad despite looking fine.

- **Land count:** 16–17 lands is the norm. With Terramorphic Expanse, land cyclers (each color has a big clunky land-cycler at common), and manifest dread smoothing, you can often get away with 16, especially in a two-color deck with fixing.

- **Must-answer two-drops exist.** Gremlin Tamer (WU: 2/2, makes 1/1 gremlin on every eerie trigger) described as "I have to kill that yesterday." Fear of Infinity (1UB: 2/2 flying lifelink, returns from graveyard on eerie) is described as "you just can't race it." Irreverent Gremlin (1R: 2/2 menace, loots when small creature ETBs) is an A-tier aggressive common. You likely cannot keep hands without a turn-two play on the draw in a format with these.

## Card notes (community consensus)

Later/settled-format takes supersede Crash Course predictions. ⚠ = prediction-vs-reality divergence noted.

### White

- [**Fear of Abduction**](https://scryfall.com/search?q=Fear%20of%20Abduction) (4WW: 5/5 flying enchantment, exile a creature you control as add'l cost, exile opp's creature, return all on leaves) — Ben's #1 white uncommon. High power. Called out as a legitimate late-game bomb.
- [**Sheltered by Ghosts**](https://scryfall.com/search?q=Sheltered%20by%20Ghosts) (1W: aura, exile opp's nonland permanent until creature leaves, enchanted creature gets +1/+1 lifelink and ward 2) — Ben: "This card is broken, top 10 uncommon in the set, going to win so many games it gets cast in." Ethan started skeptical (two-plus rating) but was sold: "I was crazy saying I could push it down." Confirmed best-uncommon status in the mid-format episode ("best uncommon in the set blah blah blah" per Ben's shorthand). ⚠ Ethan underrated at preview, both agree it's elite.
- [**Orphans of the Weave**](https://scryfall.com/search?q=Orphans%20of%20the%20Weave) (1W: 2/1, when it attacks tap any number of untapped creatures and gets +1/+1 per each tapped) — Ben's #3 white uncommon. "A house" in Green-White survival decks. Key enabler.
- [**Glimmer Seeker**](https://scryfall.com/search?q=Glimmer%20Seeker) (2W: 3/3 survival, draw card in second main if tapped and you control a glimmer creature, otherwise make a 1/1 glimmer) — Solid 3-drop, Ben's alternate #3 white uncommon, good in any white deck.
- [**Trapped in the Screen**](https://scryfall.com/search?q=Trapped%20in%20the%20Screen) (2W: exile artifact, creature, or enchantment until leaves, ward 2) — Doesn't send thing to graveyard (relevant anti-delirium tech). Both hosts noted the ward and the exile text make it significantly better than a normal O-Ring.
- [**Unsettling Twins**](https://scryfall.com/search?q=Unsettling%20Twins) (3W: 2/2, manifest dread on ETB) — "Clean bread and butter" for white decks. Both hosts had it in their top-10 commons.
- [**Seize from Slumber**](https://scryfall.com/search?q=Seize%20from%20Slumber) (4W: instant, costs 3 less if targets a tapped creature, destroy target creature) — Best in Black-White reactive decks as a premium removal spell. Little value in aggro white decks. Ben rated it as a "one" (only for BW control).
- [**Hardened Escort**](https://scryfall.com/search?q=Hardened%20Escort) (2W: 2/4, when it attacks give another creature +1/+1 and indestructible) — Clears the way for survival triggers. Flagged as GW-specific enabler.

## Signals & seat reads

- **Green being open is the premium signal.** Green is contested because it's the best color. If Say Its Name and Spine Seeker Centipede are coming to you late in pack 1, you're almost certainly in a good seat — stay green, build toward your second color.
- **Blue's commons are weak; its uncommons are strong.** If you're seeing late blue uncommons (Paranormal Analyst, Skullcap Nuisance, Fear of Falling), blue is likely open even if the commons look mediocre. Treat blue as "banger uncommons, medium commons" — don't be scared off by seeing weak common blue picks.
- **White signals caution.** Sheltered by Ghosts, Orphans of the Weave, Fear of Abduction are premium uncommons that will get taken early. If you're seeing late white uncommons, either white is open or others are avoiding it (which can be fine). Both hosts note that starting white early can pigeonhole you into Green-White or Blue-White and leave you with little ability to pivot.
- **Red-White aggro is identifiable:** if Arabella Abandoned Doll and Midnight Mayhem are coming to you, it's open but is the weakest lane. Ben tried to resist Red-White in multiple drafts because he "wanted to feel something" (more interesting mechanics) but kept ending up there or going 2-1 with it.
- **What wheels:** In the early access episode, blue was dramatically open — multiple Fear of Fallings, blue rooms, and enchantments wheeled. When a color's commons wheel entire, you are likely alone in it. However, because the format rewards synergy-building over raw-card-quality, it's worth staying patient through weak packs in a lane rather than bailing at the first sign of competition.
- **Pivot advice:** The hosts repeatedly stress: don't over-commit before pick 5–6 unless you have a premium start (e.g., Sheltered by Ghosts + early aggressive creatures in Boros, or Oblivious Bookworm + manifesting cards in Simic). Mono-colored picks (Scorching Dragonfire, Say Its Name, removal, efficient creatures) are the best picks while you read your seat. Commit to the lane when the synergistic uncommons confirm you're in a good seat.
