# Numot — general draft principles
> Evergreen, set-independent draft & play principles distilled across ~205 NumotTheNummy (Kenji Egashira) regular Arena draft VODs. Built 2026-06-10. These are expert opinion/theory — at draft time, 17Lands GIH win-rate data stays primary; treat these as the "why" behind picks.

## Picking & pick order

- Take the best card in the pack for picks 1–4 of pack 1 regardless of color. Stay maximally open until you have a "true sign" — usually around picks 5–7. "I am simply drafting the right way — take the best card out of every pack for basically the entirety of pack 1."
- Removal first, then premium creatures, then synergy pieces. "Removal's removal" — good removal is always correct to play and almost always correct to first-pick over most synergy cards.
- Two-drops are the scarcest resource; four-drops accumulate naturally. Actively draft two-drops above their stated tier when your curve is light. "Four-drops are everywhere; good two-drops are scarce."
- A two-drop with ongoing upside (card draw, explore, triggered ETB) is gold-standard. Cards that do something on turn 2 AND keep doing it later warrant first-pick consideration.
- Curve discipline over raw power. When choosing between the 5th removal spell and a two-drop, take the two-drop if the curve is light. Diminishing returns on removal past 3–4 copies are steep; missing the curve is a bigger problem.
- Don't take speculative build-around picks without a credible plan to already have (or realistically acquire) the enablers. Count your current enablers before first-picking a payoff: "how many enablers do I have for this card right now?"
- Cycling on any card upgrades it half a tier — it converts a dead card into a scry-1 draw and is never fully wasted.
- Splashing requires dedicated fixing. Taking a splash card without at least two dual lands or comparable fixing sources already in hand is a trap. "If I don't have more fixing, I will not play the green." Commit the fixing before committing the splash card.
- Don't run wraths in aggressive decks. A wrath resets your own board when you're ahead and is too slow when you're behind. Only include sweepers if the deck is built to survive to 4–5 mana and stabilize.
- Hate drafting is not worth it in normal formats; take a playable instead.
- In bot draft (Quick Draft), bots systematically undervalue certain card types (synergy engines, build-arounds). Don't try to read signals; just take the best card available and exploit what late picks the algorithm provides.
- In a format dominated by one archetype, value cheap interaction that is specifically good against the dominant deck over generic cards.

## Reading signals & staying open

- The real signal is what's *missing*, not just what's flowing. If a color is absent from packs being passed to you, someone upstream is taking it all. If you're seeing consistent late copies, that color is open.
- Wheeled cards are the most reliable signal. A premium uncommon at 8th pick or later means your whole half of the table is avoiding that color pair — act on it immediately.
- Cards that come back on the wheel signal what the player to your right is passing; cards that go unusually late late in the pack signal what your half of the table is avoiding.
- Commit to a second color by picks 4–5 of pack 1 at the latest. Staying "open" past pack 2 pick 4 is often just indecision, not flexibility. "Not even a pivot — just a weird decision."
- Pivoting mid-pack 2 is valid if your initial direction is clearly not open. The threshold is a critical mass of signals (2+ high-value cards from a new pair at pick 5+), not a single card.
- Flexibility in pack 1 is an asset; the sunk-cost fallacy leads to forcing mediocre decks. "Despite not having any [color] after those first picks I have no allegiance to it and we can pitch it real easy."
- If you opened a bomb in pack 1, you are "more or less obligated" to stay in its color. Opening a rare does not mean forcing a pair — just stay in the bomb's color and read pack 2.
- Count cards actually in the deck before switching lanes. "One, two, three, four — no, we don't have enough." Clear threshold thinking prevents half-pivots.
- When you commit to a lane, pass signals cleanly to your left so they know what's open. Know what signals you're sending even when you're taking the better card anyway.
- A "disjointed" deck that tries to do two different things executes neither well. If late in pack 2 you're split between two strategies, cut to the cleaner version.
- Late-format meta shifts matter: reevaluate pick values at end of format, not just at release. The dominant deck at format end is over-represented in the queues and should shift your removal priorities.

## Curve & deckbuilding

- 17 lands is the default for midrange and control decks. Use 16 when the curve peaks at 3, you have DFCs, cycling, looting, or non-land mana sources. Use 18 when top-heavy, splashing a shaky third color, or when multiple 4+ mana-activated abilities demand real mana. "Your curve is rather low" is a valid reason to play 16; "infinite four-drops" warrants 18.
- Connive, looting, and rummage effects justify shaving a land because they thin the deck's effective land count. "Often times it's better to run fewer lands when you have a lot of connive."
- Rummage (discard first, draw second) is more constraining than loot (draw first). Sequence discard effects with this distinction in mind — you may need to keep the land with rummage even if you'd pitch it with loot.
- Aim for 12–14 creatures minimum in most formats. Going below 12 without a specific build-around plan leads to dead-removal hands and creature-light stumbles.
- Five two-drops is a comfortable floor in aggressive and midrange decks; below three is a red flag. "I'm just hoping to pick up four more two-drops" is a recurring self-critique.
- In a two-color deck, aim for 8 sources of each color. When splashing a third color, target 7–8 sources of each main color plus dedicated fixing for the splash.
- Know which splash card justifies the mana awkwardness — usually only bombs or key unconditional removal. "The card you want to splash is usually not worth the consistency hit at the common/uncommon level."
- DFC lands and modal spells are free rolls — they occupy a land slot, not a spell slot. Take them early in any archetype.
- Cantrips, looters, and cycling lands directly reduce the flood-loss rate in BO1 builds; prioritize them in control or card-draw-dependent strategies.
- Creatures at 7–8 mana are cuttable unless they have cycling or flash. "Would rather just have a lower curve."
- Auras are risky — avoid running them unless you have two or more targets and a compelling reason.
- The 6-mana vs. 7-mana gap is larger than one turn because it compounds with land-drop variance. Prefer the cheaper version of a similar effect whenever possible.
- Going from 2 to 3 wins is the most important gem-threshold in Premier Draft. Four wins returns approximately your entry fee. Design risk tolerance around these markers; don't over-press a mediocre draft trying for a trophy.

## Play patterns & sequencing

- Lead with cheaper creatures before expensive ones when you have two plays of similar mana cost — see how the opponent responds before committing the more important threat.
- Attack first, then equip or activate abilities — avoids telegraphing and saves mana.
- Pre-combat land drop discipline: always play your land before attacking if it preserves the option to cast a spell during or after combat.
- Play tap lands at the beginning of your turn before spending mana. Work backwards from what mana you'll need by end of turn before you tap anything.
- Don't attack with utility creatures (ramp creatures, mana dorks, key engines). "I did not mean to attack with the Divide Guide because now I can't cast [spell]."
- Save removal for the right target. Using removal on a small creature in turn 3 to avoid 2 damage and then dying to their 6/6 later is the most common removal mistake. "It all came down to turn 3 when I didn't need to Dragon's Fire and I did — that was my mistake."
- Don't auto-use removal in your main phase when you can hold it at instant speed. Casting it at main phase removes information and lets the opponent adjust.
- Hold instant-speed interaction through the opponent's end step whenever possible — this denies information and maximizes options.
- When holding a combat trick, wait until attacks are declared before using it. Don't fire a pump spell in your main phase unless you need to threaten lethal that turn. "I really really really want to wait and get some extra value here."
- In races, identify whether you're the aggressor or the defender and commit. Trying to do both leads to half-measures that lose to both strategies.
- Count exact damage on both sides before every attack phase. "4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10 — we're at exactly lethal." Missed lethal from "attack mode" without prior math is a recurring punt pattern.
- Don't main-phase spells you need at end step without a reason. Missing instant-speed windows by playing too early is a consistent source of errors.
- When your deck is stabilize-then-combo, stabilize first. "You kind of need to stabilize first before you can do any of the fun stuff."
- When the opponent has no cards in hand, find the aggressive line — they cannot interact.
- If the opponent is mana-screwed, accelerate and press — do not develop value while their clock is ticking.
- Bait out blockers or removal with must-answer threats before casting your finisher. Don't jam bombs into open mana when you can wait for a tap-out window.
- Hold priority at key trigger windows (sacrifice effects, triggered artifacts). Arena will auto-pass priority and cost you value if you don't hold it manually. "Hold priority — otherwise I have a feeling the token will just sacrifice and you won't give me a chance to activate the Satchel."
- Float mana in response when lines require it. "I could have floated mana in response" is a recurring post-game regret.
- Auto-tapper is your enemy when you have triggered abilities, flip effects, or multi-use permanents. Tap lands manually in those situations.
- When bouncing your own creature, remember re-playing it costs mana. Use that to plan whether to race or stabilize.
- Verify "leaves the battlefield" vs. "when this dies" before self-bouncing — bounce fires "leaves the battlefield" triggers; dying does not.
- Don't discard your best interaction carelessly to card-draw effects. Always check whether you're throwing away the card you'll need most in two turns.
- In a losing game: identify the exact outs and play toward them explicitly rather than the hedged play that just extends the losing position.
- Identify the exact turn and decision where you misplayed. "Turn 3 was my mistake — I Dragon Fired when I didn't need to." Vague self-criticism doesn't improve your game; specific turn identification does.

## Mindset & format approach

- "Three rules of BO1: win the die roll, curve out, don't punt." These are the levers you control; macro-strategy matters less than execution.
- Play/draw is enormous in fast formats. Being on the draw against aggro opponents is a structural disadvantage equivalent to being on a worse deck. Mulligan more aggressively on the draw.
- BO1 heavily skews results toward aggro. Fast deck win rates in BO1 do not translate to BO3 / paper. When evaluating format strength, mentally adjust for the game mode.
- In BO3, sideboard specifically after game 1. "After game 1 I now know what they're playing — use that information aggressively." Bring in cards that target the opponent's specific win condition; don't just board generically.
- Cards that are "often better maindecked than sideboarded" in the right shell deserve explicit consideration during deckbuilding, not just the sideboard pile.
- Mulligans are just so bad in limited. Keep any hand with both colors represented, a relevant 2- or 3-drop, and enough land. Single-color hands with 7+ sources are mulligans. "A totally reasonable keep. A little bit risky, but..." — accept higher variance in exchange for fewer mulligans.
- One-land keeps are viable only with 2-mana cantrips or cycling effects that near-guarantee hitting land 2.
- Mana flood is a greater long-game threat than mana screw — screw creates immediate recognizable states; flood is insidious. "I've drawn 12 lands, it looked like 13 maybe."
- Know the difference between "fun to draft" and "good to draft." Explicitly flag build-arounds you're taking for fun so you evaluate the result in the right frame. "If this was an arena open or something I would probably still just take the answer."
- "Just because I don't like a card does not mean it is unplayable." Cards you find unexciting (Pack Rat, Syncopate, Piercing Light) may still be correct picks in context.
- "Good decks can lose, bad decks can win. There is some variance involved — all you can do is say that's Magic, baby, and move on." Don't tilt off one loss; re-evaluate whether the deck decision was actually wrong.
- "0-2 is the perfect start for a 7-2." After a bad early run, reset and play your best game — the loser bracket wins are still on the table.
- Flashback-format events tend to have favorable early-day conditions because players forget how to draft the format. Exploit that unfamiliarity in the first days.
- Taking breaks from a format refreshes pick-order perspective. Coming back after time off often resets misconceptions that accumulate over grinding.
